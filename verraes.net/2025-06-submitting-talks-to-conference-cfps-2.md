# Submitting talks to a tech conference

> Drawing on a decade of organizing conferences, Verraes offers guidance for writing compelling Call for Papers submissions.

## Key Facts
- An abstract's job is to filter for the right audience, not to draw in as many people as possible
- Avoid AI-generated submissions; original thinking demonstrates why your voice matters
- Expose ideas to colleagues and meetup audiences before submitting to a conference
- Reveal your actual approach rather than teasing it mysteriously
- Signal whether content is introductory or advanced so experienced practitioners can judge relevance
- Read submissions aloud to catch unclear phrasing and improve quality

## Summary
Drawing on a decade of organizing conferences, Verraes offers guidance for writing compelling Call for Papers submissions. He argues that an abstract should communicate a substantive idea clearly rather than tease it with mystery, and should help audiences self-select whether the talk is for them rather than draw the largest possible crowd. He urges speakers to write from the reader's perspective, indicate whether content is introductory or advanced, test ideas at smaller venues like meetups first, and avoid AI-generated submissions—because original thinking is precisely what demonstrates why your voice belongs on the program and serves the broader technical community.
