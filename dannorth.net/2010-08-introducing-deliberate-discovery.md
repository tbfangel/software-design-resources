# Introducing Deliberate Discovery

> North argues that traditional agile planning focused on stories, estimation, and story points addresses the wrong problem.

## Key Facts
- Ignorance is the primary constraint on project throughput, not estimation accuracy
- Teams should invest planning time in systematically identifying and reducing critical knowledge gaps
- Second-order ignorance (not knowing what you don't know) breeds dangerous overconfidence
- "Locking in our ignorance" through premature specification wastes time
- Inception should focus on mapping dimensions of potential ignorance and testing assumptions
- Create rapid feedback loops to discover and resolve constraints rather than hoping circumstances reveal knowledge
- Non-linear learning means critical discoveries arrive late, disrupting projects when assumptions prove wrong

## Summary
North argues that traditional agile planning focused on stories, estimation, and story points addresses the wrong problem. The primary constraint on project throughput is ignorance, not estimation accuracy. He uses the "street lamp analogy": like a drunk searching for keys under a lamp despite dropping them elsewhere, agile teams obsess over estimation because it's familiar and measurable, not because it's most impactful. A thought experiment suggests that repeating a completed project with accumulated knowledge would take 1/2 to 1/4 the original time, indicating that "ignorance about specific aspects of the problem at hand" fundamentally limits delivery speed. Teams face uncertainty across numerous dimensions (technology, domain, organizational dynamics, methodologies, third-party risks, interpersonal factors) and suffer from "second-order ignorance": they don't recognize what they don't know. Learning arrives in sudden insights during projects, not gradually, typically happening late and causing disruption when strongly-held assumptions prove wrong.
