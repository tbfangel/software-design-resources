# Better Best Practices

> North argues that blanket "Best Practices" initiatives often backfire by constraining experienced professionals while failing to address the real source of organizational anxiety: irrational fear disguised as risk management.

## Key Facts
- Best practices constrain experts by invalidating their hard-won instinct and intuition
- "Best practices are driven from the centre" by competent-level people who haven't experienced proficiency
- Novices need prescriptive practices; experts need flexibility and context
- Organizations mistake irrational fear for risk management when mandating rigid practices
- Alexandrian patterns provide context and multiple approaches while allowing expert judgment
- "The best Best Practices are neither Best nor Practices"
- Competent practitioners mistakenly believe expert work can be decomposed into repeatable tasks
- Different expertise levels require fundamentally different guidance approaches

## Summary
North argues that blanket "Best Practices" initiatives often backfire by constraining experienced professionals while failing to address the real source of organizational anxiety: irrational fear disguised as risk management. Applying the Dreyfus Model, he shows how different expertise levels require different guidance: Novices benefit from detailed prescriptive practices, Advanced Beginners use practices as guardrails during error-prone learning, Competent professionals define practices to eliminate mistakes they made, Proficient practitioners reference rather than follow practices, and Experts subvert practices entirely, operating from instinct. The core problem is that "best practices are driven from the centre" by competent-level people who assume decomposing expert work into repeatable tasks is possible, creating rigid systems that demoralize top performers. "You aren't just back seat driving when you constrain an expert to rules, you are invalidating their hard-won instinct and intuition." North proposes Alexandrian patterns instead: providing context, describing desired outcomes through multiple approaches, and acknowledging trade-offs.
